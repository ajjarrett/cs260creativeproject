Training Module 1: Scientific Literature




Scientific literature is at the heart of good research. To be a successful researcher, you must know what is going on in your field of interest. This will help you to find gaps in knowledge and plan your own projects. When you finish research projects, you will write about your work and inform others in the field about the latest findings. In this way, scientists are helping other scientists to solve problems and move the field forward together.

Many new researchers might feel intimidated by academic research articles because of the highly technical language. In fact, many researchers say that one of the hardest parts of joining a new research lab was understanding the language and literature of their new lab. Each discipline is almost a foreign language that must be learned.

My undergraduate research mentor believed that the best onboarding task for new undergraduate students getting involved in research is to complete a literature review, which can be written and submitted for publication. This means going through anywhere between 50-200 research studies and trying to summarize the current state of the field on some topic. This type of a project really forces a student to immerse themselves in their field.

I would recommend that as you begin reading scientific articles, read through them very slowly and thoroughly, trying to understand as much as you can. Have a highlighting tool ready to mark things and take notes as you read. For terms that are unfamiliar, look them up online and maybe watch a few YouTube videos to understand the concept at least on a surface-level. Then continue reading. After several dozen articles, you will begin to notice patterns about layout of scientific articles and themes in your chosen field of research.

Research journals can be accessed through databases, if you are at an academic institution, they likely have a portal available to access the database that they pay for. Common databases include PubMed and others. Google Scholar is another nice tool that I like to use. If you find Open Access articles, then they are not hidden behind a pay wall and anyone can read them without paying anything.
